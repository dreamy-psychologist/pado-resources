The Rest Menu

Finding rest without having to stop

Neuro-affirming

💡 Key Insight

"You need to stay still to rest" doesn't work for everyone.

Some brains are constantly processing information, which means doing nothing can actually feel harder. Without external input, internal stimuli (thoughts, sensations, discomfort) can feel even louder.

That's why what we need isn't "stopping" — it's "switching."
Moving from energy-draining activities → to energy-regulating activities.

🔄 Redefining Rest

In this menu, rest comes in three forms:

1. Low-Stimulation Rest — Reducing input without fully stopping
2. Regulatory Stimming — Repetitive sensory activities that calm the nervous system
3. Active Rest — Switching to predictable, structured activities

All three are real rest. Just pick what works for you.

🌙

Low-Stimulation Rest

Reducing input while keeping minimal comfortable activity

🎯 Purpose

To reduce external stimulation (sounds, lights, social demands) and relieve sensory overload. It's not about stopping completely — it's about creating a predictable environment where your nervous system feels "safe."

🧠 Why It Helps

Some brains process many stimuli simultaneously without filtering. After a day of social situations, sensory input, and unpredictable events, the nervous system becomes overloaded. "Doing nothing" often makes it worse — "keeping only safe stimuli" works better.

⚙️ How It Works

Predictable, controllable environments reduce amygdala (threat-detection) activation. Familiar stimuli, repetitive patterns, and solitude signal to the nervous system: "It's safe to let your guard down." That's what real rest feels like.

🏠 Creating Safe Space

Being in a dark room Wrapping up in a blanket Getting into a tent or small space Closing curtains, turning off lights Being alone in your room Staying in a familiar place

🎧 Sensory Blocking/Adjusting

Noise-canceling headphones Earplugs Sunglasses / eye mask Pulling up a hood Soft white noise Listening to familiar music on repeat

🔁 Predictable Activities

Rewatching a favorite show Rereading a favorite book Playing a familiar game Listening to the same song on loop Diving into a special interest Following a set routine

🛋️ Pressure / Proprioception

Weighted blanket Compression vest Tight clothing Squeezing between cushions Sitting with back against the wall Lying flat on the floor
✨

Regulatory Stimming

Repetitive sensory activities that stabilize the nervous system

🎯 Purpose

Stimming is a self-regulation tool for the nervous system. It helps bring down hyperarousal or raise energy from a hypoaroused state. This isn't "weird behavior" — it's how the brain takes care of itself.

🧠 Why It Helps

Repetitive, predictable sensory input sends "safety signals" to the brain. Because you control the stimulation. Suppressing stims can actually reduce your ability to regulate, increasing meltdown risk.

⚙️ How It Works

Rhythmic movements and sensory input activate the vagus nerve and promote serotonin and dopamine release. They also stimulate the vestibular (balance) and proprioceptive (body position) senses, helping you feel grounded in your body.

👐 Hand / Tactile Stims

Fidget cube / spinner Playing with a tangle Squishing slime or clay Touching favorite textures Rolling beads or stones Snapping a rubber band Finger tapping Touching fingernails Stroking soft fabric

🔄 Movement Stims

Rocking in a chair Rocking back and forth Jumping in place Spinning around Leg bouncing Hand flapping Toe walking Pacing Swinging

👀 Visual Stims

Watching a lava lamp Shaking a glitter bottle Watching water flow videos Looking at sparkly things Looking through a kaleidoscope Gazing at flames / candles Looking at patterns

👂 Auditory Stims

Listening to the same song on repeat Listening to specific sounds Humming Reading aloud Tapping out rhythms ASMR Repeating favorite quotes

👅 Oral Stims

Chewing a chewy necklace Eating crunchy foods Sour candy Chewing ice Drinking through a straw Chewing gum
🌿

Active Rest

Switching to predictable, structured activities

🎯 Purpose

To switch from stressful activities → to activities you can control. If "doing nothing" is hard, it's more effective to engage in something predictable and satisfying instead.

🧠 Why It Helps

Some brains don't like "empty space." Doing nothing can actually increase rumination, anxiety, or racing thoughts. In these cases, giving your brain "something else to do" is a better way to rest.

⚙️ How It Works

Structured activities give the prefrontal cortex (planning/execution) a clear task. This interrupts the "rumination loop" and helps you focus on the present. Activities involving hands activate the motor cortex, reducing overactivation in other areas.

✋ Repetitive Hand Tasks

Jigsaw puzzles LEGO / building blocks Beading Knitting / crocheting Coloring books Origami Model building Sorting / organizing Shuffling cards

🎮 Special Interest Deep Dives

Researching your interest Organizing collections Reading wikis Making lists of favorites Watching related videos Browsing fan communities Organizing / analyzing data

📋 Structured Activities

Making checklists Sorting / organizing items Organizing photo folders Creating playlists Planning schedules Counting / calculating Alphabetizing

🚶 Gentle Movement

Walking a familiar route Jumping on a trampoline Swimming Cycling Stretching routine Cleaning Folding laundry

🎨 Creative Activities

Drawing Writing Coding Making music Photo editing Cooking / baking

🎛️ Guide by Sensory State

😵 When Overstimulated

Too much input, exhausted, on edge

  • Reduce sensory input (dim, quiet)
  • Add pressure (weighted blanket, tight hug)
  • Low-intensity stims (slow rocking)
  • Time alone

😶 When Understimulated

Sluggish, foggy, low energy

  • Increase sensory input (bright, music on)
  • Movement stims (jumping, stretching)
  • Crunchy or sour foods
  • Splash cold water on face

🌀 When Thoughts Won't Stop

Ruminating, looping, worrying

  • Do something with your hands
  • Dive into a special interest
  • Count or sort things
  • Watch/read familiar content

💥 When Approaching Meltdown

At your limit, about to explode

  • Remove stimuli immediately (leave)
  • Go to a safe space
  • Request not to be spoken to
  • Use familiar stims

🧭 What Kind of Rest Do I Need Right Now?

😵‍💫 "It's too much. Everything is loud. People are exhausting."

Sensory overload / Social fatigue

→ Low-Stimulation Rest: Dark room, noise-canceling, alone time, weighted blanket

🔄 "I feel weird staying still. I need to do something."

Need for regulation / Energy release needed

→ Stimming: Rocking, hand stims, movement, touching favorite textures

🌀 "My brain won't stop. Thoughts keep spinning."

Rumination / Overthinking

→ Active Rest: Puzzles, organizing, special interest deep dive, hands-on tasks

😶 "I don't want to do anything. But I don't feel rested either."

Low energy / Possible dissociation

→ Gentle Sensory Input: Warm drink, favorite show on, touching comfortable textures

💜 Things Worth Remembering

1

You don't need to hide your stims.
They're how your brain takes care of itself. In safe spaces, let yourself stim freely.

2

Let go of "I should rest like everyone else."
If staying still is hard, it's not rest for you. Find what actually works for your brain.

3

Create your own "rest kit."
Keep favorite textures, fidgets, earphones, sunglasses in your bag. That way you're ready whenever you need them.

4

Rest before you hit your limit.
Don't wait until "I'm still okay" — rest when "I'm getting a bit tired." That's the best way to prevent meltdowns.

5

Deep-diving into special interests isn't "wasting time."
It's how your brain recharges. Enjoy it without guilt.

You don't have to stop to rest.

Rest in the way that works for you. 🔄
